# Ethics (WIP)

*On bearing witness without doing harm*

A street is not an abstraction. The things on it breathe, struggle, decay, and sometimes recover. To look this closely at a place is to take on an obligation toward everything living within it — not just the people who will read what we publish, but the people, animals, and quieter forms of life the work is actually about. This document exists because that obligation deserves to be written down before it's tested, not after.

## What this means in practice

- Patterns, conditions, and structural facts are published in full, including the uncomfortable ones.
- We do not publish persistent, precise, public coordinates that could be used to locate, target, or exploit a specific living being.
- Actionable detail needed to actually help someone, a precise location, a contact, a next step, lives in the case-tracking layer, accessible to whoever is acting on it, not displayed indefinitely on the public record.
- Nonhuman life on a street is documented with the same seriousness as its infrastructure and its people.
- Nothing here exists to generate engagement. If an observation doesn't serve the record or the place, it doesn't belong in it.
- This record is incomplete by design, and stays that way for a long time. That's a feature of honesty, not a flaw to apologise for.

### The record, not the registry

We document patterns and conditions. We do not surveil individual lives.

A street can be true and complete without a permanent, public, exact coordinate marking one living being. We draw a hard line between two different kinds of fact:

**Structural fact** — how often, where, what condition, for how long, and what obligation, legal or civic or otherwise, is or isn't being met. This is published in full, without softening, because softening it is its own form of harm: it lets a problem hide inside a comfortable story instead of an accurate one.

**Individually identifying operational detail** — the precise, current, persistent location of one specific person or animal. This does not belong on a public, indefinitely cached record. It belongs, if it belongs anywhere, with whoever is actually positioned to help — recorded once it's needed, not displayed indefinitely because it can be.

The difference is not how brutal the fact is. It is whether publishing it serves the record, or merely exposes someone who never consented to being found.

### No lifeform too insignificant

This work does not ration its attention by charisma, usefulness, or economic value. A stray dog, a roadside weed nobody asked about, the insects that keep a riverbank functioning — none of these are beneath documentation. Albert Schweitzer's *Ehrfurcht vor dem Leben*, reverence for life, holds that ethical seriousness extends to every will to live, not only the ones convenient to care about. We try to hold to that here, however small the thing in front of us.

### Sunlight, not exposure

Louis Brandeis wrote that sunlight is the best disinfectant, and this project exists partly because that's true. Bad patterns persist in places nobody is looking closely enough to name. We do not flinch from naming them.

But sunlight and exposure are not the same act. Journalism's own ethical tradition draws this line explicitly — minimise harm, even while pursuing the truth — and research ethics, going back to the Belmont Report, holds respect for persons as a foundation that doesn't get traded away for the sake of a complete dataset. We borrow both. The goal is a town that can see itself honestly, not a record that turns anyone's vulnerability into a fixed target.

### Data minimalism as a form of respect

We collect what the record needs, not everything it's possible to collect. This isn't a compliance exercise, though it happens to echo data-minimisation principles already written into privacy law — it's a working belief that restraint, applied deliberately, is itself a form of care. That something *can* be tracked precisely and permanently is not a reason that it *should* be.

### The seventh generation

The Haudenosaunee principle holds that every decision should be weighed against its impact seven generations out. We're not claiming that scale of foresight for a street audit in a small town on the Danube — but the spirit of it is exactly the right size for this work. The Brundtland definition of sustainability says something similar in plainer language: meeting today's needs without compromising the ability of those who come after to meet their own.

This sits naturally beside a thread already running through everything else this project touches — *mono no aware*, the quiet awareness that things change and pass, and that noticing this clearly is itself a form of care rather than despair. We are not trying to freeze Tutrakan in place. We're trying to leave behind an honest, dignified record of what was true here, in our time, so that whoever comes next inherits a place, and a record of that place, in better condition than they would have without the looking.

#### Lineage

This isn't a position invented from nothing. It leans on:

- Albert Schweitzer, *Reverence for Life*
- The Seventh Generation Principle, Haudenosaunee Confederacy
- The Brundtland Report's definition of sustainable development
- Louis Brandeis, on sunlight as disinfectant
- The Belmont Report, respect for persons
- The SPJ Code of Ethics, minimise harm
- Data minimisation as a privacy principle
- *Mono no aware* — already the aesthetic and ethical thread running through the rest of this project
